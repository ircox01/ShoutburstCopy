Will the last person working turn out the lights.

We don't mean to be alarmist and the headline might be slightly confusing because many more people are in work and everything is starting to look rosy. I am referring to the expected increase in over 65's by 20% in a decade. The whole country is turning grey.

[Daily Mail article]

Now, I don't want to be labelled as ageist, blue-rinsist or slow queue-ist. I like and respect older people and know that they are an important part of society. And I have a Mum & Dad to prove it.

But there are serious issues for us in the customer service game to consider. Older people prefer to use the telephone. Older people are less likely to use social media, mobile applications and web-chat. Older people are more likely to request Frank Sinatra as hold music. Ok, I made the last one up. The point is that call centre managers need to be aware of this change and be ready to respond to it. So rather than a headlong flight into multi-media, multi-channel and multi-problem solutions, care should be taken to think long and hard about the way we respond to the needs of a changing population. So don't ditch the phones and be prepared to work with the older generation while your competitors put up technological barriers. Because the pounds may be grey but there are lots of them and there will soon be 20% more floating around.

You have been warned. And if you don't do what we say, we'll send the old boys around.

Give your phone number, you fools …

Why is it so difficult to find a phone number when you need it? Some of the biggest brands have been in the news recently accused of hiding phone numbers in the smallest of the small print. Why would they do this to their customers?

[Article]

Well, of course there are other ways. Using the website to search for the answers you need is one ay although for too many sites the questions and answers seem to bear little resemblance to reality. Why would I want to know that xxxxxx id possible, especially when YYY is the case.

Sometimes it is good to give a company a public shaming on social media, especially is they deserve it. Tweet something dramatic, retweet it to everyone you know and stand back for the results. Child's play.

Or send SMS or email and hope that they appear on someones desk at some stage this side of Christmas.

But for many transactions, the humble phone call is still the best way of communicating and big business would be wise to bear that in mind. For a phone call includes the world of relationship building, upselling and problem solving unlike any other. It also includes long queues, agents who seem to struggle to stay awake and phrases like 'it's company' policy but let's ignore the downsides for now when the upsides are more appealing and pertinent to the article.

Give your customers the phone number, sort out your processes and work with your agents to look at how to add value with every call. It might not be cheap but done right it will bring far more value to your organisation. Of course, if you do it badly then you might as well hide it in the smallest of small print.

Gamification will not succeed according to Gartner

Motivating agents is not easy. Anyone who has managed a call centre will know that every agent is different, everyone has their own trigger points and will all respond differently to applied motivational stimuli – or, as it is sometimes known, the agent of the week mug. But gamification is the next big thing – or is it?

http://www.gartner.com/newsroom/id/2251015

If you have tried to motivate a team of agents by running competitions then you will know the fear that the backbiting, competitive nastiness and ineffectual results that are the result of a game that has taken most of the weekend and a multitude of coloured pens to design and implement. No wonder some call centre managers jump when the word incentive is shouted behind their backs.

But gamification removes the need for flipcharts, balls, pens and hats and allows you to pay for access to systems that give agents a chance to win stuff … for doing their job. Fantastic innovation and one that is sure to continue to grow, whatever Mr Gartner says.

But whether it succeeds or not, and how effective it is deemed to be, does not mask the need for great management to produce great motivation. Agents that are motivated by being trusted, given responsibility and empowerment, and are regularly made aware that the company appreciates their input might not need outside stimulation. Everyone else might wish their managers would stop playing games. Watch this space as the gamification debate rages.

Who knows better, you or the customer?

On your marks, get ready, go! They're off. The customer service manager streaks ahead, making decisions as they go, pushing agents into greater productivity as they go towards the line. But on the outside comes the customers shouting and screaming, demanding better service. But at the last minute they are forced into a queue, and the call centre manager wins – they know best.

Why listen to customers when you have professionals to make decisions? Read on

Great companies listen to their customers. There are many examples where this has been the case. http://www.forbes.com/sites/alanhall/2013/05/17/listening-to-customers-yields-success/ gives you a flavour.

In one way, the question should be 'why wouldn't you'. Well, for one reason, customers are crazy and angry and there are too many of them*. Well some of them are. Perhaps even lots of them. But the point is, asking customers creates a lot of work and they are not experts. If you listened to customers you would be giving away all of your products, delivering them to the second and then giving away some money in compensation because the instructions didn't mention that leaving the computer in the rain for 24 hours might make even Windows 8 a little shaky.

Of course, it is much simpler than this. There are some basic steps. 1) Put in a customer satisfaction survey that asks the questions and gives you the information you need in an easy to interpret format and 2) Use this information to help the company grow. Because although suggestions to paint your head office pink or demands for agents to speak Spanish are perhaps not to be taken seriously, if you have a mass of customers telling you that they hate your packaging, surely it is time to factor their opinions in.

So the smart manager lets the customer win, steal all of their ideas and make them much more loyal in the process. Now that is victory.
** we love customers really but are not above shameslessly using them to make our points

Customers fighting back – hooray

Everyone has their worst nightmare. For me it involves cliffs and sharks, and hopefully if I stay well away from the sea it will remain just a nightmare. For the average Call Centre Manager, it is likely to either be 4000 calls in the queue with all agents at lunch, or being strapped down in a chair and made to listen to customer complaints. And for the pessimist amongst us, both.

According to the news that latter nightmare is more likely than ever

http://www.corpcommsmagazine.co.uk/news/3672-more-uk-consumers-are-standing-up-for-customer-service-standards

Customers are standing up for their customer service rights. Now, I am not 100 sure what a 'customer service right' is. The right to have your name pronounced correctly? The right to never be cut off during a heated conversation? The right to speak to a supervisor when asked rather than being told the supervisor doesn't have a mind of their own or that the agent in question is suddenly the manager in charge, and no one else is there. What a co-incidence.

But back to the point. Customers are telling it how it is. More often and probably louder. But this is a good thing. It is. Yes, it is. Do you want me to shout? It is. Because if customers that are not happy are also not good for business. We all know the statistics. One angry customer will tell eighteen others, including the butcher, the baker etc. And then they will leave and buy everything elsewhere. In summary, not good. But dealing with the customer complaint well is very much the flipside. Customers love us – and we have learnt something.

So when customers shout at us listen carefully, fight the urge to shout 'hooray' and then use the information to the best of your ability to improve. Done well, there will be less shouting but more business. Done badly, you will have a real fight on your hands.
